Materials Lab Interface

For the UT School of Architecture’s Materials Lab

Objective

To create an interface that shows the total sustainability of building materials and lets users compare building materials.

Context

The Materials Lab maintains the largest and most comprehensive academic material collection in the world with over 27,000 material samples.

They asked for concepts for a user interface to help students, professors, and professionals research and compare the properties and life cycles of building materials.

Contextual Researh

I began by asking questions about the audience and learning about material science.

A Professor

Richard is a professor at the University of Texas and is quite the materials science expert. Earlier this week he read a study about a particular kind of clay that could improve indoor air quality.

He uses the Materials Lab Database to gather additional information about the clay to determine how he can incorporate it into an experiment he is conducting with similar materials.

A Student

Lauren is an undergrad student at Texas Tech studying architecture. She is considering the energy costs of building materials for her design project.

She isn't sure where to begin because she only has a basic understanding of material science. Her professor recommends the Materials Lab Database to explore a range of options.

A Professional

Matt is an architectural engineer working at a small firm in the Austin area. He needs to find an insulating material with marginal hazards to humans and the environment.

He knows a few, but uses to the Materials Lab Database explore other materials, compare them, and consider their properties.

Narrative Presentation

An initial concept presented data according to a material's life cycle rather than by category.

Presenting Information

With a generous amount of data to present, I sought ways to compare data without an overwhelming experience and gravitated towards designs that avoided burying data.

Task Analysis

I expected the user's needs to compare a material in two ways—first against the library of materials and then against a group of selected materials. Analysis of material data was a secondary task.

Guiding Lines

Continuous lines representing a material guides a user and ties information together visually, but risked being confusing.

Level One

The first level shows properties for a total of 14 material families ranked least to greatest. Wood and ferrous metals are currently selected.

Level Two

Using a menu or the graph, users can explore deeper within a material family such as these elastomers. At each side are the other material families ranked above and below elastomers.

Level Three

Exploring even further, a user can see properties of manufactured materials by companies.

Making Refinements

After presenting the concepts to a group of stakeholders and receiving feedback, I went back to refine the interface.

One change was breaking apart the information into breathable sections for easier consumption.

Recognizing Over Recall

I was conscious to allow users to recognize selected materials rather than recalling them. I carried this out by properly placing titling and using tags.

Title

As before, the user is exploring through different layers of information. The graph and menu synchronously update together and the currently selected material updates on the top bar.

Future Design

The scope of this project looked at the presentation of data and navigation to compare materials. Additional tasks might include adding and removing materials from a list and data analysis.
